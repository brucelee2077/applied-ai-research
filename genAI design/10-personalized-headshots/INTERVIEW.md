# Chapter 10: Interviewer Guide — Personalized Headshots (DreamBooth/LoRA)

## Opening Problem Statement

"Design an AI headshot generation system — users upload 10-20 selfies, and within 30 minutes receive 50 professional headshot images in various styles. The system must serve 100,000 users per day, preserve identity reliably, and have safeguards against misuse for deepfakes. I want to go deep on the prior preservation loss design, how you'd select LoRA rank for quality vs. efficiency, what diagnostic signals tell you the model is overfitting vs. underfitting, and how you'd scale the system to millions of users with per-user adapters."

---

## Probing Question Tree

### Area 1: Prior Preservation Loss — What Catastrophic Forgetting Looks Like

**Surface:** "The README explains prior preservation loss. Why do you need it?"

**Probe 1:** "Without prior preservation, DreamBooth causes two distinct problems: catastrophic forgetting and language drift. Distinguish between them precisely — give a concrete example of each failing in a headshot system."
> *Looking for:* Catastrophic forgetting: the fine-tuned model can no longer generate plausible images for the class concept ('a photo of a person') because the U-Net weights have been overwritten to generate only the specific subject. Concrete: after finetuning on John's face, prompt 'a photo of a person in a suit' generates John's face regardless of context. Language drift: the model's understanding of the text embedding space shifts — the rare token [V]='sks' starts to absorb semantic meaning that corrupts adjacent concepts. Concrete: after finetuning on John's face with prompt 'a photo of sks person,' the word 'person' in the model's cross-attention weights has shifted toward John-specific features, causing prompts like 'a person holding a briefcase' to sometimes generate John-like features even without the [V] token.

**Probe 2:** "Prior preservation loss uses class-generated images as regularization: L_total = L_subject + λ × L_prior. The README sets λ=1.0. What happens at λ=0 (no prior preservation) vs. λ=10 (aggressive prior preservation), and how would you tune λ for a user with a distinctive face (e.g., very unusual eye color, distinctive scar)?"
> *Looking for:* λ=0: no regularization → model quickly overfits to subject's features. After 1000 steps, all generated images strongly resemble the subject even without the identifier token. Language drift severe. λ=10: prior regularization dominates → model preserves general face generation well but learns the specific subject's identity less strongly. Generated headshots may look like 'a person who vaguely resembles John' rather than a strong identity match. Tuning for distinctive features: a user with very distinctive features (strong prior deviation from the mean face) needs lower λ to let the model learn the deviation from the average face. Diagnostic: generate 'a photo of sks person' (with identifier) and 'a photo of a person' (without). If the latter starts looking like the subject, λ is too low. If the former doesn't capture the distinctive feature, λ is too high.

**Expert probe:** "Prior preservation uses images generated by the original, unfinetuned model as class regularization images. An alternative approach (PerDiff, custom diffusion) uses real images from the class (scraped from the web). Compare these two sources of regularization data on: (1) quality of regularization, (2) privacy and legal risks, (3) distributional coverage of edge cases."
> *Looking for:* Model-generated regularization (standard DreamBooth): quality depends on the base model's own generation quality — low diversity if the base model has limited coverage of faces/styles. Privacy/legal: uses only model-generated images, no real people's data involved → clean from a data privacy standpoint. Coverage of edge cases: inherits the base model's biases (overrepresentation of certain face types), may not regularize well for underrepresented demographics. Real scraped images: quality — real images have the diversity and naturalness that model-generated images lack. Privacy/legal: significant risk — scraping real people's faces requires consent, creates deepfake risk, violates GDPR in many jurisdictions. Coverage: captures the true diversity of the class, including underrepresented groups. Production choice: model-generated is safer legally and practically, but invest in improving base model diversity. Real images only if you have a licensed dataset with explicit consent.

### Area 2: LoRA Rank Selection — How to Choose r

**Surface:** "The README uses LoRA rank 4-8. How do you decide?"

**Probe 1:** "LoRA with rank r adds matrices A ∈ ℝ^(d_out × r) and B ∈ ℝ^(r × d_in). For a U-Net attention projection with d_in = d_out = 768, compare the parameter count for r=1, r=4, r=8, and r=64, and explain what the 'effective rank' of the weight update ΔW = A×B tells you about what the model can learn."
> *Looking for:* Parameter count: r=1: 768×1 + 1×768 = 1,536 params. r=4: 768×4 + 4×768 = 6,144 params. r=8: 768×8 + 8×768 = 12,288 params. r=64: 768×64 + 64×768 = 98,304 params (12.5% of the original 768×768=589,824 params). Effective rank: ΔW = A×B has rank at most r. This means the update to the weight matrix lives in a subspace of dimensionality r. For personalization: the concept of 'this specific face' requires encoding a direction in the weight space that differs from the average face. If the identity direction in the weight space is low-dimensional (it often is — a face can be characterized by a few key features), low rank (r=4-8) is sufficient. When low rank fails: complex identities with many unusual features simultaneously (e.g., distinctive glasses, beard, hair color, scar) may require higher rank because each feature adds an independent dimension to the deviation.

**Probe 2:** "How would you decide between r=4 and r=64 empirically, and what diagnostic metrics would you monitor during training to detect when low rank is insufficient?"
> *Looking for:* Empirical comparison: train both and measure identity preservation (ArcFace score), text alignment (CLIP-S), and image quality (FID or human evaluation) on a held-out test set with prompts the model wasn't trained on. Diagnostic metrics during training: (1) Reconstruction loss on training images: if r=4 model has significantly higher reconstruction loss than r=64 at convergence, low rank is underfitting. (2) Identity score on training images (should be high for both): if r=4 is significantly lower, rank is too low. (3) Generalization test: generate 20 images with prompts that weren't in training. If r=4 shows identity degradation on unseen prompts but r=64 doesn't, r=4 is underfitting. Rule of thumb: start with r=4, increase only if reconstruction loss on training images remains high or identity scores are below threshold. The diminishing returns of increasing rank beyond 16 are usually not worth the storage and inference cost increase.

**Expert probe:** "LyCORIS and other advanced LoRA variants propose alternatives to standard LoRA for diffusion personalization: LoHa (LoRA with Hadamard product), LoKr (Kronecker product), and others. What is the fundamental limitation of standard LoRA that these variants address, and for a headshot system serving 1M users, would you use them?"
> *Looking for:* Standard LoRA limitation: ΔW = A×B has rank at most r regardless of the target matrix structure. For weight matrices that are not well-approximated by a low-rank update (e.g., those encoding non-linear transformations), the LoRA decomposition is suboptimal. LoHa: ΔW = (A₁ × B₁) ⊙ (A₂ × B₂) (Hadamard product of two low-rank matrices). This allows capturing multiplicative interactions between weight dimensions, providing more expressive updates with the same parameter count. LoKr: uses Kronecker product structure, better for weight matrices with inherent block structure. For 1M user headshot system: practical considerations dominate. Standard LoRA at r=4-8 is well-understood, has broad tool support (Kohya, diffusers), and requires ~10-50MB storage per user. Advanced variants like LoHa improve quality by ~5-10% at similar parameter counts but increase implementation complexity and debugging difficulty. Recommendation: use standard LoRA for production stability; adopt LoHa/LoKr for power users or premium tiers where quality matters more than simplicity.

### Area 3: Overfitting vs. Underfitting in DreamBooth — Diagnostic Signals

**Surface:** "How do you know if the model is overfitting or underfitting to the training images?"

**Probe 1:** "A user complains their generated headshots 'all look the same' even when the prompts specify different outfits, settings, and lighting. This is a sign of overfitting. Describe exactly which components of the U-Net have been overfit, and what the loss curves would look like vs. an underfit model."
> *Looking for:* Overfitting signs in U-Net components: (1) Cross-attention: the attention maps for the [V] token have become overly narrow — they activate on specific face textures learned from training images and ignore the conditioning prompt. (2) Self-attention: the model learned specific pose and composition patterns from the training images. Loss curves: overfit model — training reconstruction loss drops very low (<0.01), but validation loss (on a held-out augmented version of training images) plateaus or increases. Underfit model: both training and validation reconstruction loss remain relatively high. The 'all look the same' complaint specifically indicates that the text conditioning has been weakened — the [V] token's embedding has overwhelmed the other text tokens in cross-attention. Fix: reduce training steps, increase prior preservation λ, use content images for the identifier token to avoid strong style anchoring.

**Probe 2:** "You add random horizontal flipping and random crops to augment the 15 training images. After training, you notice the generated headshots show the subject's face mirrored/flipped inconsistently. What's happening and how do you fix it?"
> *Looking for:* Random horizontal flipping creates inconsistency: if 50% of training images are flipped, the model sees both the original face orientation and its mirror. For a face that is not bilaterally symmetric (most faces have slight asymmetries), the model learns both orientations equally, causing generated images to sometimes produce the mirrored face. Fix: (1) Disable horizontal flipping for face personalization (unlike object recognition tasks, faces have meaningful handedness). (2) If flipping is used, add left/right face indicators in the training captions ('facing left' vs. 'facing right'). (3) Face alignment to canonical orientation before training — align all training images to a standard pose so the model sees consistent orientation. This is a subtle point that distinguishes someone who has actually run DreamBooth training from someone who just read about it.

**Expert probe:** "DINO score and ArcFace are both used to measure identity preservation. In your production evaluation pipeline, you compute both for a batch of headshots. You find DINO score is high (0.8) but ArcFace score is low (0.3). What is the model producing, and what does this reveal about the different properties each metric captures?"
> *Looking for:* DINO score high / ArcFace low: DINO captures visual structural similarity (ViT self-supervised features) — the generated images look visually similar to the reference in terms of overall appearance, pose, lighting, and texture. ArcFace captures face identity specifically — it measures how similar the face embeddings are in face-recognition space. DINO high + ArcFace low: the model is generating images that have the right hair color, skin tone, general face shape (captured by DINO), but the specific identity markers (face geometry, proportions, distinctive features) are wrong. This is the 'looks vaguely like the person' failure mode. Causes: (1) Prior preservation was too strong (λ too high), averaging the specific identity toward the mean face. (2) LoRA rank too low — couldn't capture the specific identity direction. (3) Training images were too varied in pose/lighting, causing the model to learn average appearance rather than specific identity. Fix: lower λ, increase rank, add more consistent frontal-view training images.

### Area 4: Production System — Rate Limiting and Abuse Prevention

**Surface:** "How would you prevent misuse of this system for deepfakes?"

**Probe 1:** "A user uploads 20 images of a celebrity. Your system doesn't know this. What signals would a production system monitor to detect and prevent celebrity face generation, and at what point in the pipeline do you intervene?"
> *Looking for:* Detection signals: (1) Face identity check against a celebrity database (ArcFace embedding similarity vs. a known-celebrity embedding store). (2) Image reverse search against known celebrity photos. (3) User behavior patterns: users uploading high-quality paparazzi-style photos (vs. selfies with typical selfie metadata). Pipeline intervention points: (a) At upload time: face recognition against celebrity database — reject if similarity > threshold. (b) During training: if the trained LoRA adapter produces a face that matches a celebrity when decoded (post-training check). (c) At output time: NSFW classifier + celebrity recognition on generated images. Limitation: the celebrity database must be maintained and current. Novel celebrities or public figures not yet in the database won't be caught by face recognition.

**Probe 2:** "Scale the system to 100,000 users per day with LoRA training required for each user. Each LoRA training takes 30 minutes on an A100 GPU. How many GPUs do you need minimum, and what's your job queuing strategy to meet the 30-minute SLA?"
> *Looking for:* GPU calculation: 100,000 users/day × 30 min/user = 3,000,000 GPU-minutes/day = 50,000 GPU-hours/day. At 24 hours/day: 50,000/24 ≈ 2,083 A100 GPUs minimum. This is 2,000+ GPUs. Cost: at $3/hour per A100: $150,000/day. This is why most production systems use LoRA at much lower rank (r=2 or even r=1) to reduce training time to <10 minutes. Or use textual inversion which is simpler but lower quality. Queuing strategy: priority queuing with SLA monitoring — users who submit during off-peak hours get served faster, peak-hour users get queued with estimated completion time. Preemptive scaling: monitor queue depth and auto-scale cloud GPU instances. Spot instances for 50-70% cost reduction at the risk of job interruption (must implement checkpoint/resume).

**Expert probe:** "Your system uses identity verification (ArcFace clustering) to confirm all uploaded selfies are of the same person. A user attempts to upload 15 images of themselves and 5 images of another person to blend identities. What does the ArcFace clustering output look like, and how does your system detect and handle this multi-identity attack?"
> *Looking for:* ArcFace clustering: compute ArcFace embeddings for all 20 images, then cluster using DBSCAN or K-means. A single-identity submission: all 20 embeddings cluster into one tight cluster (cosine similarity > 0.6 between all pairs). Multi-identity (15+5) submission: two clusters emerge — one with 15 images, one with 5. Detection: (1) Cluster count > 1 → reject with message "we detected multiple faces in your submission." (2) Maximum pairwise dissimilarity check: if any two images have ArcFace cosine similarity < 0.4, they likely don't belong to the same person. (3) Cluster size threshold: if any cluster has < N images (e.g., < 5), the training data for that identity is insufficient — reject. Additional defense: require consent verification (user must certify these are photos of themselves), and log the IP/account for pattern analysis.

## Red Flags to Watch For

- **Prior preservation loss = data augmentation.** Doesn't understand that it's specifically preventing catastrophic forgetting of the class concept.
- **LoRA rank is a memory vs. quality tradeoff only.** Cannot explain the rank vs. expressiveness argument.
- **DINO = ArcFace for identity evaluation.** Cannot distinguish what each metric captures.
- **"Use more training data" to fix identity.** Doesn't understand that 10-20 images is by design and more images without quality checks can hurt.
- **"Filter prompts" as the only deepfake prevention.** Doesn't propose face identity checking at upload or output stages.
- **Language drift and catastrophic forgetting are the same problem.** Cannot precisely distinguish them.
