# Staff / Principal Engineer Interview Guide — Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

---

## How to Use This Guide

This guide is designed for interviewers assessing Staff-level and Principal Engineer candidates on their ability to design, build, evaluate, and operate production-grade Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems. RAG is one of the most consequential patterns in modern applied AI: it grounds LLM outputs in retrieved evidence, dramatically reducing hallucination, enabling fresh knowledge without costly retraining, and producing auditable citations — capabilities that are now table stakes in enterprise AI products.

**Who this guide is for.** Use it for candidates interviewing for roles that require end-to-end ownership of ML-powered search, knowledge assistants, enterprise chatbots, developer tools (e.g., Copilot-style), or any product where LLM outputs must be traceable to a source corpus.

**How to run the interview.** The guide is organized around the canonical 7-step ML system design framework: Requirements, ML Framing, Data, Model Development, Evaluation, System Design, and Deployment. Each step contains one or more interview questions. After the structured steps, a set of Deep Technical Probes stress-tests retrieval-specific depth. You do not need to cover every question in a single session. For a 60-minute interview, choose Steps 1–4 plus two probes. For a 90-minute panel covering two interviewers, split Steps 1–4 and Probes to one interviewer and Steps 5–7 and remaining Probes to the other.

**How to score.** Each question provides four tiered answer descriptors — No Hire, Weak Hire, Hire, and Strong Hire — each containing 4–8 sentences describing what a candidate at that tier would typically say. The tiers reflect both technical depth and engineering judgment. A Staff Engineer is expected to operate comfortably at Hire level across all steps and hit Strong Hire on at least two areas of demonstrated expertise. A Principal Engineer is expected to consistently hit Strong Hire across evaluation, system design, and at least two deep probes, and should demonstrate cross-functional leadership instincts (tradeoffs, cost modeling, incident response) throughout.

**Red flags and hiring decision summary tables appear at the end of this document.** Review them before calibration discussions.

## Step 1 — Requirements Clarification

### Q1. You are asked to build a RAG system for a large enterprise that wants employees to ask questions against a 5-million-document internal knowledge base. What clarifying questions do you ask before designing anything?

**No Hire:** The candidate jumps directly into a technical solution without asking any clarifying questions, or asks only a single superficial question such as "what language is it in?" They have no structured approach to eliciting requirements. They treat all RAG problems as identical and immediately propose a generic architecture without understanding the use case. They conflate product requirements (what users need) with technical parameters (what the system must do). They do not ask about latency, scale, accuracy, freshness, or compliance, and they cannot articulate why these dimensions matter. They may ask about model preference rather than about the problem constraints.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate asks about document format and scale but misses important dimensions such as latency targets, accuracy vs. speed tradeoffs, or compliance requirements. They ask about "what kind of documents" but do not follow up on whether the corpus is static or live. They may ask about budget but not frame it in terms of indexing cost vs. query cost vs. LLM call cost. They identify some requirements but cannot explain how different answers would change the architecture. They are reactive rather than proactive — they need prompting to uncover latency or PII concerns.

**Hire:** The candidate asks a structured set of questions covering at least five critical dimensions: (1) document modality and format (PDF, web pages, code, images, scanned documents), (2) corpus size and update frequency (static vs. streaming updates), (3) latency requirement (real-time chat under 2 seconds vs. asynchronous overnight synthesis), (4) accuracy vs. recall tradeoff (is missing an answer worse than a wrong one?), (5) citation and traceability requirements (do users need to see source documents?), and (6) PII and data governance constraints. They explain how each answer changes design decisions — for example, PII in documents changes whether you can use a third-party embedding API or need an on-premises model. They ask about feedback signals and how success will be measured.

**Strong Hire:** In addition to the above, the candidate proactively asks about the query distribution (factoid questions vs. multi-hop analytical questions vs. document summarization), because this changes the entire architecture — simple factoid queries can be answered with a bi-encoder + BM25 hybrid, whereas analytical questions may require multi-hop retrieval or agentic loops. They ask whether the system needs to handle adversarial users or prompt injection. They ask about the organizational structure: who maintains the corpus? Is there a curation team, or is it fully automated? They flag the latency tension — sub-2-second real-time chat is hard if the corpus is 5 million documents with complex re-ranking, and they already propose the approximate shape of a solution (tiered retrieval) before committing to a design. They also ask about the failure mode preference: is it better to say "I don't know" than to hallucinate? This question alone reveals deep understanding of RAG's core risk surface.

### Q2. A product manager says "we need real-time chat with sub-2-second response and we also need perfect recall across 10 million documents." How do you resolve this tension?

**No Hire:** The candidate does not recognize a tension. They say "we can use a good LLM and it will be fast and accurate." They have no awareness that retrieval latency, re-ranking latency, and LLM generation latency stack, or that ANN search becomes more expensive at 10 million documents. They propose no tradeoff analysis and cannot articulate what levers are available. They may suggest simply "using a faster server."

**Weak Hire:** The candidate recognizes there is a tension but can only propose one lever, such as "use a smaller model." They understand that speed and accuracy conflict but cannot enumerate the specific pipeline components that contribute to latency. They may suggest caching without explaining what is safe to cache. They cannot quantify the tradeoffs or propose how to measure whether a given configuration meets both constraints.

**Hire:** The candidate explicitly decomposes end-to-end latency into its components: query embedding (~10–50ms), ANN search (~10–100ms depending on index and hardware), re-ranking (100–500ms for cross-encoder on top-100), prompt construction (negligible), and LLM generation (500–1500ms depending on output length and model size). They identify re-ranking as the most expensive adjustable component and propose a tiered retrieval approach: bi-encoder retrieves top-100 fast, then cross-encoder re-ranks top-10. They discuss ANN index tuning (HNSW ef-search parameter controls recall vs. latency tradeoff). They note that "perfect recall" is a product requirement that needs to be translated into a measurable metric (e.g., Hit Rate@10 > 95%) rather than taken literally. They propose establishing an offline evaluation harness to measure recall at different speed points.

**Strong Hire:** The candidate reframes the requirement negotiation as a product conversation, not just a technical one. They explain that "perfect recall at 10 million documents in under 2 seconds" is physically achievable but has real cost implications (high-memory HNSW index, beefy re-ranker hardware, premium LLM tier with low P99 latency), and they ask the PM to prioritize. They introduce the concept of approximate Pareto frontier: at a given recall level, there is a minimum latency achievable given hardware budget, and they propose measuring this empirically before committing. They also flag that latency is a distribution, not a mean — they ask about P50 vs. P99 latency targets, because the tail matters for user experience. They may propose a degraded-mode fallback: if re-ranking is too slow, skip it and serve the bi-encoder result with a confidence indicator. They bring up corpus update lag as a separate dimension: real-time indexing for a 10-million-document corpus is non-trivial and may affect both latency and consistency.

## Step 2 — ML Framing

### Q3. How do you formally frame a RAG system as a machine learning problem? What are the inputs, outputs, and objective?

**No Hire:** The candidate cannot articulate a formal ML framing. They describe RAG only at the product level ("you retrieve documents and then the LLM answers") without connecting it to any ML objective. They do not distinguish the retrieval component from the generation component. They cannot articulate what the model is being trained or optimized to do. They may confuse RAG with fine-tuning, treating them as equivalent approaches to injecting domain knowledge.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate recognizes that RAG has two components — retrieval and generation — and can describe each at a high level. They may say "the retriever finds relevant documents and the LLM generates an answer from them." However, they cannot articulate the formal objective of either component, do not mention contrastive training for the embedding model, and cannot explain the difference between fine-tuning, RAG, and prompt engineering in terms of when each is appropriate.

**Hire:** The candidate frames the full system formally. The offline phase is: parse documents → chunk → embed via bi-encoder → build ANN index. The online phase is: embed query → ANN search for top-K chunks → optional re-ranking → prompt construction (system prompt + retrieved context + user query) → LLM generation → post-processing. They frame the retriever as a relevance ranking problem: given query q and candidate document set D, score each (q, d) pair and return the top-K highest-scoring documents. They frame generation as conditional text generation: P(answer | query, context_1, ..., context_K). They articulate the tradeoff between fine-tuning (bakes knowledge into weights, expensive, stale after training cutoff), RAG (knowledge is external and fresh, cheaper to update, but retrieval can fail), and prompt engineering (fastest to iterate, but only works for knowledge that fits in context).

**Strong Hire:** The candidate provides a unified information-theoretic framing. The core objective is to maximize P(correct answer | query) by maximizing the probability that the correct supporting evidence is both retrieved (retrieval recall) and correctly conditioned upon (generation faithfulness). They note that errors compound multiplicatively: if retrieval recall at K is 80% and generation faithfulness is 90%, end-to-end correctness is at most 72%. This framing motivates independent optimization of each stage. They discuss the knowledge freshness dimension: fine-tuning encodes a static snapshot of knowledge into model weights, making updates require retraining; RAG externalizes knowledge so updates only require re-indexing. They note the hallucination surface: a fine-tuned model can hallucinate from memorized but incorrect training data; a RAG model can hallucinate by ignoring retrieved context or retrieving wrong context. They also introduce RAFT (RAG-Aware Fine-Tuning) as a hybrid: fine-tune the LLM to correctly use retrieved context and explicitly ignore distractor documents, improving faithfulness without sacrificing freshness.

## Step 3 — Data

### Q4. Walk me through a production document processing pipeline. How do you go from raw PDFs and web pages to a searchable vector index?

**No Hire:** The candidate's answer is too high-level to be actionable ("you extract the text and embed it"). They have no awareness of the challenges in document parsing — scanned PDFs, tables, figures, headers/footers, multi-column layouts. They treat all documents as plain text and have no strategy for chunking. They cannot explain what an embedding is or why chunking matters. They do not mention index construction at all.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate understands that text must be extracted, chunked, and embedded, but they apply a single generic strategy to all document types (e.g., fixed 512-token chunks with no overlap for everything). They do not discuss parsing challenges for complex documents (tables, figures, scanned images). They know vector databases exist but cannot name any specific ones or compare their tradeoffs. They mention overlap chunking but cannot explain why overlap helps or hurts.

**Hire:** The candidate covers the full pipeline with concrete choices at each stage. For parsing: rule-based extractors (PyMuPDF, pdfplumber) work well for programmatic PDFs; AI-based layout detection (Unstructured.io, Azure Document Intelligence) is needed for complex layouts, tables, and scanned documents requiring OCR. For chunking: they discuss fixed-length chunking (simple, fast, ignores semantics), overlap chunking (reduces boundary artifacts at 10–20% overlap), semantic chunking (split on sentence boundaries, group semantically coherent units), and structure-aware chunking (respect HTML headers, markdown sections, code blocks). They justify chunk size: 256–512 tokens is typical for QA tasks; too small loses context, too large dilutes the embedding signal. For embedding: Sentence-BERT, E5-large, GTE as general-purpose options; domain fine-tuning when in-domain data is available. For indexing: Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant, or pgvector depending on scale, operational preference, and whether hybrid search is needed.

**Strong Hire:** The candidate adds several depth indicators. They discuss metadata extraction alongside text: document ID, source URL, creation date, author, section headings, and document type — all of which enable metadata filtering at retrieval time to avoid semantic drift (e.g., filtering to documents updated in the last 90 days). They explain parent-document chunking: store small chunks for precise embedding and retrieval but return the parent chunk or full document to the LLM for generation context, because small chunks embed well but large chunks provide better generation context. They discuss multimodal documents: for PDFs with figures, either use a multimodal embedding model (CLIP-style) or generate text descriptions of figures via a vision-LLM and embed those descriptions. They address incremental indexing: for a live corpus with frequent updates, a streaming pipeline (Kafka → document processor → embedding service → vector store upsert) is needed; they discuss the trade-off between batch rebuild (simpler, consistent index state) and incremental updates (lower latency, but can leave stale or orphaned embeddings). They also flag the deduplication problem: if the same document appears in multiple sources, duplicate chunks inflate retrieval scores and waste index space.

### Q5. A new batch of 50,000 documents arrives every night. How do you update the vector index without disrupting live search traffic?

**No Hire:** The candidate suggests "rebuild the index every night and restart the service." They have no awareness of the downtime this would cause, the cost of re-embedding 5 million existing documents, or strategies for zero-downtime updates. They cannot distinguish between an update to a vector index and an update to a database table.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate suggests using a staging index but cannot describe the mechanics of a blue-green index swap. They know that incremental updates are desirable but cannot explain how vector databases handle upserts, how to handle deleted documents, or how to maintain index consistency during a rolling swap. They cannot estimate the cost of nightly re-embedding 50,000 documents.

**Hire:** The candidate proposes a concrete zero-downtime strategy: maintain a "shadow" index that receives incremental nightly upserts. Once the nightly batch is fully indexed and validated (offline evaluation on a held-out query set), swap the read pointer from the active index to the shadow index. For documents that are updated, upsert by document ID — the vector store replaces the old embedding. For deleted documents, issue explicit deletes. They discuss the validation gate: before the swap, run automated retrieval quality checks (Hit Rate@10 on a canary query set) to catch regressions from malformed document batches. They discuss cost: embedding 50,000 documents at 512 tokens each is approximately 25M tokens of embedding inference, which is cheap enough to run nightly. They acknowledge that for truly streaming corpora (documents arriving continuously), the batch approach introduces lag and a message-queue-based pipeline is preferable.

**Strong Hire:** The candidate additionally addresses tombstoning and eventual consistency. When a document is deleted, the embedding in the vector store must be explicitly removed, otherwise deleted content will still surface in retrieval. They propose a soft-delete pattern: mark documents as inactive in a metadata store and filter them out at query time until the next index rebuild removes them cleanly. They discuss segment-level updates in HNSW indexes: some vector databases support online index updates with maintained graph connectivity, but others require periodic graph optimization to prevent degraded recall over many incremental inserts (because the HNSW graph becomes suboptimal). They propose a monthly full index rebuild alongside nightly incremental updates to prevent recall degradation from graph fragmentation. They also mention monitoring: track the embedding freshness distribution (timestamp of indexed version vs. source document timestamp) as a production health metric, and alert when lag exceeds SLA.

## Step 4 — Model Development

### Q6. Compare bi-encoders and cross-encoders for retrieval and re-ranking. When do you use each?

**No Hire:** The candidate cannot distinguish between a bi-encoder and a cross-encoder. They may know what BERT is but cannot explain how it applies to retrieval. They have no awareness of the latency/quality tradeoff between these architectures. They suggest "use the best model" without any awareness of deployment constraints.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate knows that bi-encoders are "faster" and cross-encoders are "more accurate" but cannot explain mechanistically why. They cannot describe the inference-time operation of either architecture. They know that there is a two-stage pipeline but cannot justify the specific cutoffs (why retrieve top-100 with bi-encoder, not top-10 or top-1000, before cross-encoder re-ranking). They have not encountered ColBERT or late interaction architectures.

**Hire:** The candidate provides a clear mechanistic comparison. Bi-encoder (e.g., Sentence-BERT, E5): encodes query and document independently into fixed-size dense vectors; similarity is computed as dot product or cosine similarity at retrieval time. Document embeddings are precomputed and stored in the vector index. Inference time per query: one forward pass for the query embedding, then a fast ANN lookup — effectively O(log N) with HNSW. Cross-encoder: concatenates query and document as a single input sequence, runs a full BERT forward pass, and outputs a relevance score. Cannot precompute document representations; requires one forward pass per (query, document) pair at query time. Much more accurate because the model can attend to interactions between query tokens and document tokens, but O(N) at inference time. Two-stage architecture: bi-encoder retrieves top-100 candidates fast (~20ms), cross-encoder re-ranks to top-10 (100–500ms for 100 candidates) — quality close to cross-encoder, latency acceptable. They explain why re-ranking only top-100 is safe: bi-encoder recall at 100 is typically 90–95%, so the cross-encoder is unlikely to find relevant documents outside that set.

**Strong Hire:** The candidate introduces ColBERT as a middle ground and explains the MaxSim scoring mechanism precisely. ColBERT generates per-token embeddings for both query and document at training time. Document token embeddings are stored offline (larger storage than bi-encoder but still precomputable). At query time, per-token query embeddings are computed, and the relevance score is the sum over query tokens of the maximum cosine similarity between each query token and all document tokens. This late interaction mechanism captures more semantic nuance than a single vector bi-encoder without requiring a full forward pass over (query, document) pairs. They discuss the storage tradeoff: ColBERT uses roughly 128 × token_count floats per document vs. a single 768-float vector for a bi-encoder. They discuss domain adaptation: for specialized corpora (legal, medical, code), fine-tuning the bi-encoder with in-domain (query, relevant_document, irrelevant_document) triplets using InfoNCE/NT-Xent contrastive loss typically yields 5–15% improvement in Hit Rate@10. They know how to generate hard negatives: mine BM25 top results that are not relevant — these are more informative than random negatives and produce a better-calibrated retriever.

### Q7. Walk me through how you would fine-tune an embedding model for a specialized domain (e.g., legal contracts).

**No Hire:** The candidate cannot describe a fine-tuning process for embedding models. They may suggest fine-tuning a generative LLM instead. They do not know what contrastive learning is or why it is used for embedding training. They cannot explain what a training pair looks like for a retrieval task.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate knows that fine-tuning requires (query, positive document) pairs but cannot explain how to construct hard negatives or why they matter. They may describe a classification fine-tuning approach (fine-tune BERT on relevant/not-relevant labels) without recognizing this as suboptimal compared to contrastive learning. They cannot estimate the data requirements for effective fine-tuning.

**Hire:** The candidate describes a complete fine-tuning pipeline. Data construction: collect or annotate (query, positive_doc) pairs from domain data — user search logs with clicks, expert-annotated QA pairs, or synthetic data generated by prompting an LLM to generate questions a given document would answer. Hard negative mining: retrieve top-K candidates with a base bi-encoder or BM25; take documents that are highly ranked but not the ground-truth positive — these teach the model to distinguish near-relevant from truly relevant. Training objective: InfoNCE loss (in-batch negatives + hard negatives) on a base model like E5-base or SBERT. Training details: small learning rate (1e-5 to 2e-5), linear warmup, 2–5 epochs, evaluate on held-out retrieval task every epoch. Evaluation: Hit Rate@10, MRR on a domain-specific test set. They discuss the risk of catastrophic forgetting: fine-tuning too aggressively on domain data can hurt general retrieval performance, so multi-task training or a low learning rate with early stopping is preferred.

**Strong Hire:** The candidate discusses several advanced considerations. Synthetic data generation using an LLM: given a document chunk, prompt GPT-4 to generate three plausible questions that would be answered by this chunk — dramatically reduces annotation cost and can scale to millions of training examples. Augmented SBERT: use a cross-encoder teacher to label (query, document) pairs with soft relevance scores, then distill these scores into a bi-encoder student — combines the quality of cross-encoder judgments with the inference speed of a bi-encoder. Domain-specific tokenization: if the domain uses specialized vocabulary (legal citations, contract clause identifiers), consider whether the base model's tokenizer handles these well or if vocabulary extension is warranted. Evaluation on OOD queries: test not just on held-out queries from the same distribution as training, but on queries from new departments or use cases — generalization is more important than in-distribution performance at Staff+ level. Production deployment consideration: they discuss embedding model versioning — when you update the embedding model, ALL existing document embeddings must be recomputed and the index rebuilt, because old and new embeddings are not comparable. This makes embedding model updates operationally expensive and motivates a deliberate model update cadence.

## Step 5 — Evaluation

### Q8. What metrics do you use to evaluate a RAG system end-to-end? Explain the distinction between faithfulness and correctness.

**No Hire:** The candidate mentions only one metric, typically accuracy or BLEU, without any RAG-specific framing. They cannot distinguish between retrieval quality and generation quality. They treat RAG evaluation as identical to standard NLP model evaluation. They cannot give a concrete example of what a faithfulness failure looks like vs. a correctness failure.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate knows that retrieval and generation should be evaluated separately and can name some metrics (recall, precision for retrieval; BLEU or ROUGE for generation). They may be aware of RAGAS but cannot explain what it measures. They can give a basic definition of faithfulness ("the answer should be based on the retrieved documents") but cannot give a concrete example of the faithfulness-correctness distinction or explain why they diverge.

**Hire:** The candidate describes a four-dimensional evaluation framework. (1) Context Relevance: measures retrieval quality — did we retrieve the right chunks? Metrics: Hit Rate@K (fraction of queries where at least one relevant chunk is in top-K), MRR (mean reciprocal rank of first relevant result), NDCG@K (graded relevance). (2) Faithfulness: measures whether the generated answer is grounded in the retrieved context — is every claim in the answer supported by at least one retrieved chunk? Evaluated using an NLI model to check entailment between answer claims and context. (3) Answer Relevance: measures whether the answer addresses what was asked — semantic similarity between the question and the answer (answers that are faithful but off-topic fail this). (4) Answer Correctness: measures factual accuracy against ground truth — exact match or F1 against a reference answer. They give the key distinction: a faithful answer is one supported by retrieved context; a correct answer is one that is factually true. These diverge when the retrieved document is outdated (faithful but wrong) or when the LLM adds correct information not in the retrieved context (correct but unfaithful).

**Strong Hire:** The candidate provides concrete examples of both divergence cases and explains the production implications. Faithful-but-wrong example: the retrieved document says "the warranty period is 1 year" — the LLM faithfully generates an answer citing this — but the policy was updated to 2 years and the vector index has a stale document. The system fails the user despite being a "good" RAG system from a faithfulness perspective. This motivates index freshness monitoring and document timestamping. Correct-but-unfaithful example: the user asks "what are the side effects of Drug X?" The retrieved context mentions nausea and fatigue. The LLM correctly adds headache (which is a real side effect) from its parametric memory, but this information is not in the retrieved context — the response is correct but unfaithful. This is dangerous because the same mechanism that allows the LLM to add correct information also allows it to add incorrect information with no retrieval signal. They discuss production monitoring strategy: faithfulness can be monitored continuously with a lightweight NLI model (no ground truth required); correctness requires ground truth labels and is evaluated on a monthly human-annotated sample stratified by retrieval confidence score. They mention that retrieval confidence scores (distance to nearest neighbor in embedding space) are a useful proxy for knowledge gap: low-confidence retrievals are more likely to result in both unfaithful and incorrect answers.

### Q9. How do you design an offline evaluation harness for a RAG system before you have real user query logs?

**No Hire:** The candidate says "you can't evaluate without user data" or suggests deploying to production and measuring user satisfaction as the primary evaluation method. They have no strategy for pre-deployment validation. They cannot propose a method for constructing a labeled evaluation dataset.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate suggests manually writing 20–30 test questions. They acknowledge the need for labeled data but have no systematic approach to generating it at scale. They cannot connect the evaluation harness to a specific metric framework or explain how it would drive decisions about chunking strategy, embedding model choice, or prompt design.

**Hire:** The candidate proposes a concrete strategy for building a pre-deployment evaluation set. Synthetic query generation: for each document chunk in the corpus, prompt an LLM to generate 2–3 plausible questions that are answered by that chunk — this produces a large labeled (query, relevant_chunk) dataset cheaply. Expert annotation: supplement with 100–200 hand-crafted queries covering edge cases (ambiguous queries, queries requiring multi-hop reasoning, queries about recently updated documents). Evaluation harness: for each test query, run the full pipeline, record top-K retrieved chunks, record the generated answer. Compute Hit Rate@K, MRR, NDCG for retrieval quality; run faithfulness NLI scoring for generation quality. Use this harness to A/B test chunking strategies, embedding models, and prompt templates. They discuss the limitations of synthetic queries: they tend to be cleaner and more literal than real user queries, so they overestimate production performance.

**Strong Hire:** The candidate adds adversarial evaluation design and discusses the importance of stratified evaluation. Adversarial cases to include: (1) queries where BM25 retrieves the right document but dense retrieval misses it (tests hybrid search value), (2) queries that require combining information from two documents (tests multi-hop), (3) queries about topics not in the corpus (tests "I don't know" behavior), (4) queries with ambiguous entities (tests attribute-level precision). Stratified evaluation: separate the test set by document type, recency, query type (factoid vs. analytical), and query frequency — evaluate each stratum separately to identify which failure modes matter most at product scale. They also discuss using a holdout set for model selection and a separate test set for final reporting, to avoid overfitting to the evaluation set. They mention the RAGAS framework as a standard tool that implements these four dimensions with LLM-as-judge options where NLI models lack coverage.

## Step 6 — System Design

### Q10. Design the full online inference pipeline for a production RAG system. What happens between when a user submits a query and when they see the answer?

**No Hire:** The candidate describes a two-step pipeline: "retrieve documents, then send to LLM." They have no awareness of the intermediate components (safety filtering, query rewriting, re-ranking, output filtering, citation generation). They cannot estimate the latency budget for each component. They treat the online pipeline as a single monolithic operation.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate describes a multi-step pipeline but omits critical components such as query safety filtering, query expansion, or output safety. They know about embedding and retrieval but have not thought about edge cases (what happens if no relevant documents are found? what if the query is ambiguous?). They discuss caching only at a superficial level ("cache popular queries") without specifying what to cache and at what layer.

**Hire:** The candidate describes a complete online pipeline: (1) Query safety filter: detect and block harmful queries (prompt injection, PII extraction attempts, out-of-scope requests) using a lightweight classifier or rule-based filter. (2) Query expansion/rewriting: optionally rewrite the query using HyDE (generate a hypothetical document that would answer the query, embed that instead) or step-back prompting (reframe a specific question as a more general one to improve retrieval coverage). (3) Query embedding: encode the (rewritten) query using the bi-encoder. (4) ANN search: retrieve top-K (K=50–100) candidate chunks from the vector index, optionally combined with BM25 for hybrid search. (5) Metadata filtering: optionally apply hard filters (e.g., document recency, document type) before or during ANN search. (6) Re-ranking: cross-encoder re-ranks top-K to top-10. (7) Prompt construction: format retrieved chunks as context in the LLM prompt, including source metadata. (8) LLM generation: generate answer with faithfulness instructions ("answer only from the provided context"). (9) Output safety: detect and filter hallucinated claims, PII in output, or policy violations. (10) Citation generation: map answer sentences back to source chunks and surface source metadata to the user.

**Strong Hire:** The candidate enriches each stage with production-grade details and discusses failure modes. For HyDE: the LLM generates a hypothetical document that bridges colloquial query language to document language (e.g., a user asks "why is my Python code slow" and HyDE generates a document excerpt about Python performance optimization, which embeds more similarly to actual performance documentation than the original colloquial query). They note HyDE's failure mode: if the LLM hallucinates in the hypothetical document, retrieval is guided by hallucination, which can surface irrelevant or harmful content — they propose confidence-gating HyDE (only apply it when base retrieval returns low-confidence results). For caching: query-level caching (exact match on normalized query string) plus embedding-level caching (if a semantically similar query was embedded recently, reuse the embedding) plus retrieval-level caching (if the same top-K chunks were retrieved recently, reuse them). They discuss cache invalidation: retrieval cache must be invalidated when the underlying index is updated. They describe the "no relevant documents found" fallback: if all retrieved chunks have similarity scores below a threshold, return a graceful "I don't have information about this topic" response rather than generating from parametric memory, which reduces hallucination risk.

## Step 7 — Deployment, Monitoring, and Operations

### Q11. How do you monitor a production RAG system? What signals tell you the system is degrading before users complain?

**No Hire:** The candidate suggests monitoring only user-facing metrics like CSAT scores or thumbs up/down feedback. They have no awareness of leading indicators (signals that predict degradation before users notice) vs. lagging indicators (signals that reflect degradation after users have experienced it). They cannot propose any automated monitoring beyond error rates.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate suggests monitoring retrieval latency and error rates but misses retrieval quality degradation (which can occur with no latency or error signal — the system returns wrong documents just as fast as correct ones). They know that embedding model updates require index rebuild but cannot describe a monitoring strategy for embedding freshness or index health.

**Hire:** The candidate describes a multi-layer monitoring stack. Infrastructure layer: p50/p95/p99 retrieval latency, embedding service throughput, LLM API error rate, token budget utilization. Retrieval quality layer: MRR on a held-out canary query set run nightly — this catches index drift from document updates or embedding model changes. Faithfulness layer: run a lightweight NLI model on a sample of production (retrieved context, generated answer) pairs daily, track the distribution of NLI scores, and alert on downward shifts. Cost layer: embedding API calls per day, LLM API calls per day, average input/output token count per query (rising input tokens may indicate prompt bloat). Index freshness layer: track the distribution of indexed document timestamps vs. source document timestamps, alert when median lag exceeds SLA. User feedback layer: explicit thumbs up/down, implicit signals (query reformulations immediately after an answer suggest dissatisfaction).

**Strong Hire:** The candidate proposes an end-to-end monitoring philosophy that distinguishes leading from lagging indicators and integrates them into an incident response playbook. Leading indicators (catch problems early): retrieval similarity score distribution (if the average max-similarity score for queries drops, it may indicate new query types or corpus staleness), "I don't know" response rate (increase may indicate corpus gap or retrieval failure), query volume by topic (sudden spike in a topic not well covered by the corpus). Lagging indicators (confirm problems): NLI faithfulness score, user thumbs-down rate, escalation to human support rate. A/B testing strategy: run chunking strategy experiments (e.g., fixed-length vs. semantic chunking) as online A/B tests using interleaved result evaluation — randomly assign queries to pipeline variants and compare faithfulness + user satisfaction scores. Feedback loop from corrections: when a support agent corrects a wrong answer, log the (query, correct answer, retrieved chunks) tuple as a negative training example for the retriever and as an annotation for future evaluation. They also discuss the "data flywheel": as user queries accumulate, use them to enrich the evaluation set and drive continuous improvement of the embedding model.

## Deep Technical Probes

### PROBE 1: Retrieval Failure Despite Semantic Similarity

**Q: Your RAG system retrieves "domestic orders refund policy" when the user asks "international orders refund policy." Both documents are about refund policy — the semantic similarity is high. Why does this happen and how do you fix it?**

**No Hire:** The candidate cannot diagnose the root cause. They suggest "use a bigger embedding model" or "add more training data" without any mechanistic analysis. They do not understand that embedding models can collapse attribute-distinguishing features when those attributes are less common in training data. They cannot explain what hybrid search is or why it would help here.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate correctly identifies this as an embedding failure ("the model doesn't distinguish 'international' from 'domestic'") but can only propose a single fix (use a different embedding model or fine-tune). They cannot explain the mechanism of BM25 hybrid search or how it would solve this specific problem. They know what metadata filtering is but cannot connect it to this failure mode.

**Hire:** The candidate diagnoses the root cause precisely: embedding models trained on general corpora learn to represent topical similarity (both documents are about "refund policy") while discarding attribute-distinguishing features (international vs. domestic) because these attributes appear less frequently and carry less weight in the contrastive training signal. The fix has three components: (1) Hybrid search: combine dense retrieval (captures topical similarity) with BM25 (exact term matching — will score "international orders refund policy" much higher for the query "international orders refund policy" because it contains all four exact terms). Typical fusion via Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) or linear interpolation of scores. (2) Metadata filtering: if documents are tagged with scope (international, domestic, all regions), apply a hard filter based on detected entities in the query. (3) Query rewriting: preprocess the query to identify key discriminating entities and ensure they appear prominently. They discuss HyDE as an alternative: generating a hypothetical document about international refund policy would itself contain "international" prominently, which would improve the dense retrieval signal.

**Strong Hire:** The candidate provides a deeper analysis of when hybrid search helps and when it does not, and proposes an evaluation methodology for diagnosing this class of failure. They explain that BM25 is a bag-of-words model with TF-IDF weighting and BM25 document length normalization — it excels at exact keyword matching but fails at synonymy (query: "cancel my order" vs. document: "order cancellation"). Dense retrieval fails at attribute precision (as above). The hybrid combination covers both failure modes. They propose a diagnostic: compute BM25-only results and dense-only results for a sample of query failures — if the correct document appears in BM25 top-5 but not dense top-5, this is a dense-retrieval attribute failure, and hybrid search will fix it. If it appears in neither, the document does not exist in the corpus and the failure is a corpus coverage gap. They discuss HyDE's failure mode in this context: if the LLM generating the hypothetical document also confuses international and domestic policies (which it might if the query is ambiguous), HyDE will compound the retrieval error rather than fixing it. This motivates using HyDE only as a second-pass strategy when base retrieval returns low-confidence results.

### PROBE 2: Multi-Hop Retrieval

**Q: A user asks "which products sold by a company that partnered with Acme Corp in 2023 are eligible for our extended warranty?" A single retrieval step will not find the answer. How do you design a system that handles this?**

**No Hire:** The candidate suggests "retrieve more documents" (increase K) or "use a longer context window" without recognizing that the fundamental issue is sequential dependency — you need to first find Acme's partners, then look up their products, then check warranty eligibility for those products. They cannot decompose the query into sub-queries.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate recognizes this as a multi-hop problem and suggests "do multiple retrievals" but cannot describe a principled mechanism for generating the intermediate queries. They may suggest having the LLM decompose the query first and then retrieve for each sub-query, which is a valid approach, but they cannot describe IRCoT or ReAct or compare their tradeoffs.

**Hire:** The candidate describes two principled approaches. IRCoT (Interleaved Retrieval with Chain of Thought): the LLM generates one reasoning step at a time, each step generates a new retrieval query, the retrieved documents are appended to the context, and reasoning continues. Example: step 1 reasoning = "I need to find which companies partnered with Acme Corp in 2023" → generates retrieval query "Acme Corp 2023 partnerships" → retrieves partnership documents → step 2 reasoning = "The partners are Company X and Company Y — now I need to find their products" → retrieval query "Company X products" → and so on. ReAct: the LLM explicitly outputs structured actions (Thought / Action: search[query] / Observation: [results]) in a loop. Compared to IRCoT, ReAct is more explicit and easier to debug but requires the LLM to follow a strict format. Both incur multiple LLM calls per query (2–5x latency overhead). They propose a complexity classifier: route simple factoid queries to single-step RAG and complex multi-hop queries to IRCoT/ReAct to avoid paying the latency cost for simple queries.

**Strong Hire:** The candidate provides nuanced analysis of the tradeoffs and failure modes. IRCoT's failure modes: (1) error propagation — if step 1 retrieval is wrong, all subsequent steps are conditioned on wrong information; (2) stopping criterion — need a principled way to decide when enough evidence has been retrieved (typically a confidence score from the LLM or a fixed maximum number of hops); (3) context window growth — each hop appends more retrieved text, and long contexts degrade LLM attention quality (lost-in-the-middle problem). Mitigation: summarize retrieved evidence at each hop rather than appending raw text. ReAct vs. IRCoT distinction: ReAct uses explicit tool call syntax (the search query is a formatted action), making it easier to integrate with tool-use frameworks and easier to audit; IRCoT uses the partial reasoning chain as an implicit retrieval signal, which can be more natural but less controllable. They propose a hybrid: use IRCoT for 2-hop queries where the intermediate entity is predictable, and escalate to ReAct with human-in-the-loop for 3+-hop queries where hallucination risk is high. They also discuss graph-based retrieval as an alternative for highly structured knowledge domains: build a knowledge graph from documents and do graph traversal instead of multi-step dense retrieval, which gives more reliable multi-hop paths at the cost of graph construction complexity.

### PROBE 3: Cross-Encoder vs. Bi-Encoder at Scale

**Q: Someone proposes replacing the bi-encoder + cross-encoder two-stage pipeline with a single cross-encoder that directly re-ranks all documents. What is the problem with this, and what does a 2-stage pipeline actually cost at scale?**

**No Hire:** The candidate agrees with the proposal or cannot identify the problem. They may say "cross-encoders are just better so we should use them." They have no awareness of the quadratic or linear scaling problem with cross-encoders at retrieval scale. They cannot estimate BERT inference cost or latency.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate identifies that cross-encoders are "too slow" but cannot quantify the problem or propose specific solutions. They know that the two-stage approach exists but cannot justify the specific K values (retrieve top-100, re-rank top-100) or explain why bi-encoder recall at 100 is sufficient to safely discard the rest.

**Hire:** The candidate provides concrete cost analysis. A cross-encoder re-ranking all 10 million documents in the corpus requires 10 million BERT forward passes per query. At a typical cross-encoder throughput of 200 pairs/second on a GPU, this would take 50,000 seconds per query — completely infeasible. Even re-ranking top-1,000 candidates takes 5 seconds. The 2-stage pipeline: bi-encoder ANN search retrieves top-100 in 20–50ms (HNSW log-N lookup); cross-encoder re-ranks top-100 in approximately 500ms (100 BERT forward passes at 200 pairs/sec with batching — in practice, batching 100 pairs together is efficient and takes 200–500ms on a GPU). Total online latency budget: 50ms embedding + 50ms ANN + 400ms re-ranking + 700ms LLM = 1200ms, within a 2-second budget. Justification for K=100 cutoff: empirically, bi-encoder Hit Rate@100 is typically 90–95%, meaning the correct document is in the top 100 for 90–95% of queries — the cross-encoder then identifies the correct one from this shortlist.

**Strong Hire:** The candidate discusses ColBERT as a third architectural option that fills the quality-cost space between bi-encoder and cross-encoder, and provides a nuanced cost-benefit comparison. ColBERT stores per-token document embeddings offline (for a document of 200 tokens, stores 200 vectors of dimension 128 = 25,600 floats vs. 768 floats for a bi-encoder — roughly 33x larger storage per document). At query time, ColBERT computes per-token query embeddings (one forward pass, like a bi-encoder) and then computes MaxSim scores: for each query token, find the maximum cosine similarity with any document token, then sum across query tokens. This is more expensive than a single dot product but far cheaper than a full cross-encoder forward pass, and quality is consistently between the two. At 10 million documents with average 200 tokens, ColBERT index size is 10M × 200 × 128 × 4 bytes ≈ 1TB — feasible on cloud storage but expensive. They propose using ColBERT as the re-ranker stage (replacing the cross-encoder) when latency is critical and the cross-encoder is too slow. They also discuss learned sparse retrievers (SPLADE) as an alternative that achieves cross-encoder quality with bi-encoder inference speed by learning to produce sparse, high-dimensional term-weighted representations.

### PROBE 4: RAG vs. Long-Context LLMs

**Q: A colleague argues that with 2-million-token context windows now available, RAG is obsolete — just stuff all the documents into the context. How do you respond?**

**No Hire:** The candidate either agrees entirely ("yeah, long context makes RAG obsolete") or disagrees dogmatically without any analysis ("RAG is always better"). They cannot quantify the gap between context window size and corpus size, or enumerate the specific failure modes of long-context stuffing.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate correctly identifies that large corpora exceed context windows but cannot calculate the specific numbers or identify more nuanced failure modes like lost-in-the-middle. They know that long-context LLMs are expensive but cannot estimate cost per query or compare it to a RAG pipeline.

**Hire:** The candidate provides a concrete quantitative argument and a nuanced "when to use which" framework. Corpus size arithmetic: 50,000 documents × 1,000 tokens/document = 50 million tokens — 25× the 2-million-token context window. Even for smaller corpora that fit, there are multiple problems: (1) Lost-in-the-middle: LLMs exhibit primacy and recency bias — information in the middle of a long context is retrieved with significantly lower accuracy than information at the beginning or end. This was empirically demonstrated at 2024 Stanford and has been confirmed across multiple model families. (2) Cost: a 2M-token context window call costs approximately 100× more than a 20K-token RAG call at current API pricing. For a system handling 10,000 queries/day, this is the difference between $100/day and $10,000/day. (3) Latency: time-to-first-token scales with input context length; 2M-token inputs have TTFT on the order of 10–30 seconds. (4) Frequent updates: if the corpus is updated daily, the context would need to be reconstructed daily for every query. When long-context wins: full-document analysis (the task requires understanding relationships across the entire document, not just retrieving a fact), very small static corpora, rare-entity queries where retrieval misses specialized terms.

**Strong Hire:** The candidate introduces the shard-and-merge failure mode for corpora that exceed even the largest context windows. If you shard a 50-million-token corpus into 25 × 2M-token windows and run the LLM on each shard separately, you get 25 answers that must be merged — the merge step itself requires reasoning across 25 potentially conflicting partial answers, which is another LLM call and may lose cross-shard dependencies (if the correct answer requires combining information from shard 3 and shard 17, no single shard LLM call will produce it). They also discuss the emerging hybrid approach: use long-context for the final generation step (pass top-20 retrieved chunks totaling 20K tokens into a long-context LLM for richer synthesis) while still using RAG for initial retrieval to avoid the cost and lost-in-the-middle problem. They articulate the principle: RAG and long-context are complementary, not competitive — retrieval reduces the context to the relevant subset, long-context enables rich reasoning over that subset. They predict that as context window costs decrease, the optimal K in top-K retrieval will increase, but full-corpus stuffing will remain economically and quality-inviable for large corpora.

### PROBE 5: Faithfulness vs. Correctness in Production

**Q: In production monitoring, how do you detect and distinguish faithfulness failures from correctness failures? What do you do when you detect each?**

**No Hire:** The candidate cannot define faithfulness or correctness precisely. They treat them as synonyms for "the answer is wrong." They have no strategy for detecting either in production and cannot propose a monitoring approach that does not require manual annotation of every query-answer pair.

**Weak Hire:** The candidate can define faithfulness ("grounded in retrieved context") and correctness ("factually accurate") but conflates the monitoring approaches. They suggest using human review for both, which is too expensive for production scale. They cannot describe an NLI-based faithfulness monitoring approach or explain why faithfulness monitoring is cheaper than correctness monitoring.

**Hire:** The candidate provides separate monitoring strategies for each dimension. Faithfulness monitoring (no ground truth required): for each production (retrieved context, generated answer) pair, run a lightweight NLI model to check whether the answer claims are entailed by the retrieved context. Decompose the generated answer into atomic claims (e.g., using an LLM), then for each claim, check entailment against the retrieved chunks. Track the distribution of entailment scores and alert on downward shifts. Failure mode: NLI model domain gap — if the NLI model was trained on general-domain text, it may make incorrect entailment judgments on specialized domains. Mitigation: fine-tune the NLI model on domain examples. Correctness monitoring (requires ground truth): build a factual database for the most common query types (e.g., product specifications, policy terms) and use exact-match or LLM-as-judge to compare generated answers against ground truth. Run on a sampled 1–5% of queries weekly; stratify by retrieval confidence score (low-confidence retrievals are oversampled). Alert when correctness drops in specific topic clusters.

**Strong Hire:** The candidate discusses the production decision loop that connects monitoring signals to remediation actions. For faithfulness failure (NLI score drops suddenly): first check if the index was recently updated — a batch of poorly parsed documents or documents with misleading snippets can cause the retriever to return plausible but non-entailing context. Second, check if a new query pattern has emerged that returns low-similarity results (retrieval confidence distribution). Third, check if the LLM version changed (different instruction following behavior). Remediation: if it is a corpus issue, re-parse and re-index the affected document batch; if it is a retrieval issue, increase K and tighten the cross-encoder threshold; if it is an LLM issue, add explicit faithfulness instructions to the prompt ("answer only from the provided context; if the context does not contain the answer, say you don't know"). For correctness failure: requires deeper investigation because root causes span retrieval (wrong documents retrieved), corpus (documents are outdated or incorrect), and generation (LLM ignores retrieved context or hallucinates). The candidate proposes a attribution protocol: for each incorrect answer, determine which component failed by running ablation — does providing the correct document in context produce the correct answer? If yes, retrieval is the failure point; if no, generation is the failure point. They also discuss the "I don't know" rate as a safety valve: if the system is configured to abstain when retrieval confidence is low, a rising "I don't know" rate is an early warning that corpus coverage is degrading relative to the query distribution.
