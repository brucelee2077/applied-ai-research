# Chapter 09: Interviewer Guide — Text-to-Image (Diffusion Models)

## Opening Problem Statement

"Design a production text-to-image generation system. I want to go deep on the mathematical foundations of diffusion models — specifically the variational lower bound objective and what it actually optimizes — plus the mechanics of classifier-free guidance, why guidance scale > 7 causes artifacts, and how you'd make architecture choices between U-Net and DiT in 2024. I'll also push on safety and the practical limits of CLIP as a text encoder."

---

## Probing Question Tree

### Area 1: Forward/Backward Process and the Variational Lower Bound

**Surface:** "Explain how diffusion models work mathematically."

**Probe 1:** "The training objective is MSE between true noise ε and predicted noise ε_θ. But where does this come from? Derive the connection between this noise prediction objective and the variational lower bound (ELBO) that's actually being optimized."
> *Looking for:* The ELBO for the joint distribution over the noisy sequence: log p(x_0) ≥ ELBO = E_q[-log p_θ(x_0|x_1)] + Σ_t KL(q(x_{t-1}|x_t, x_0) || p_θ(x_{t-1}|x_t)). Ho et al. 2020 showed this simplifies to: L_simple = E_{t,x_0,ε}[||ε - ε_θ(x_t, t)||²]. The connection: the KL divergence terms between the reverse and forward posteriors reduce to comparing the predicted noise to the true noise because both are Gaussian and the variance is fixed. The mean of q(x_{t-1}|x_t, x_0) is a function of x_0 and x_t, and predicting ε is equivalent to predicting x_0 (they're related by the reparameterization). Strong candidate explains that predicting ε (v-prediction or x_0 prediction are alternatives) and that the choice affects the signal-to-noise balance across timesteps.

**Probe 2:** "The noise schedule β_t controls how much noise is added at each step. What is the difference between linear and cosine noise schedules, and why do cosine schedules produce better results for high-resolution generation?"
> *Looking for:* Linear schedule: β_t increases linearly from β_1 to β_T. Problem: at high-resolution images (e.g., 512×512), the signal-to-noise ratio drops very rapidly in the early timesteps — almost all information is destroyed in the first few steps. The model spends most of its training on the fully-noisy end, where there's nothing useful to learn. Cosine schedule: designed so that the signal-to-noise ratio decreases smoothly on a cosine curve, ensuring the model has meaningful training signal throughout the full noise range. Formally: ᾱ_t = cos²(πt/2T), which gives a much smoother SNR decay. For high-resolution: the linear schedule's rapid information loss is more severe at high resolution because there are more high-frequency components that are destroyed immediately. Stable Diffusion v1 used linear; v2+ and later models use cosine or learned schedules.

**Expert probe:** "Min-SNR weighting is a training technique introduced in 2023 that reweights the per-timestep loss contributions. Explain what Min-SNR does, why the standard unweighted loss causes imbalanced training, and what the practical effect on generation quality is."
> *Looking for:* Standard L_simple averages losses equally across all timesteps t. Problem: the SNR (signal-to-noise ratio) varies enormously across timesteps. At low t (mildly noisy), the model must make fine-grained predictions → large gradients, strong learning signal. At high t (heavily noisy), the prediction problem is nearly impossible → small gradients, weak learning signal. Min-SNR weighting: cap the per-timestep loss weight at min(SNR_t, γ), where γ is a hyperparameter (e.g., γ=5). This prevents high-SNR timesteps from dominating training while ensuring that very high-noise timesteps still contribute. Practical effect: faster convergence, better quality at both high and low noise levels. Models like SD3 and FLUX use this weighting. Strong hire knows that this is related to the 'loss weighting' in diffusion literature and connects it to flow matching which uses a different (more uniform) timestep weighting.

### Area 2: Classifier-Free Guidance — Why High Guidance Causes Artifacts

**Surface:** "The README explains CFG at a high level. What's actually happening mathematically?"

**Probe 1:** "CFG formula: ε_guided = ε_uncond + w * (ε_cond - ε_uncond). The README says w > 20 causes 'oversaturated artifacts.' Derive the mathematical reason why high w causes saturation, starting from what the guidance formula actually represents as a score function."
> *Looking for:* The guidance formula is: ε_guided ∝ ε_uncond + w * (ε_cond - ε_uncond). Interpreting as score functions: the score is ∇_x log p(x|c) ≈ w * ∇_x log p(c|x) + ∇_x log p(x). High w: the gradient of the conditional log-likelihood ∇_x log p(c|x) dominates. The DDPM denoising step uses this guidance to move x toward high-likelihood regions of the text-conditioned distribution. Problem: at high w, the gradient can push x outside the natural data manifold — the update direction maximizes text alignment at the cost of image naturalness. Concretely: the model overshoots from x_t to x_{t-1}, landing outside the support of p(x). This manifests as: (1) color saturation (pixel values pushed to extremes), (2) texture collapse (features like hair become smooth blobs), (3) face distortion. Strong hire connects this to adversarial examples — maximizing any score too aggressively pushes toward out-of-distribution artifacts.

**Probe 2:** "Rescaling guidance (also called CFG++ or guidance rescaling) is a technique that mitigates artifacts at high guidance scale. Explain the core insight and how it modifies the standard CFG formula."
> *Looking for:* Core insight: high guidance scale magnifies the noise prediction, which effectively changes the variance of the predicted x_0. When ε_guided has larger magnitude than ε_uncond, the predicted x_0 has lower variance (it's more 'committed'). Guidance rescaling: after computing ε_guided, rescale it to match the variance of ε_cond: ε_rescaled = (std(ε_cond) / std(ε_guided)) × ε_guided + (1 - std(ε_cond)/std(ε_guided)) × ε_cond. This preserves the guidance direction while preventing magnitude explosion. Result: allows higher effective guidance scale without artifacts. Used in SDXL and later models. Strong hire knows that this is related to the dynamic thresholding in DALL-E 2 (clip pixel values to [-1, 1] during generation, then rescale) which addresses the same artifact.

**Expert probe:** "Perturb-and-Average Scoring (PAAS) and Attending to Relevant Tokens are two recent approaches to address 'prompt neglect' — the common failure where diffusion models ignore some words in the text prompt. Explain why prompt neglect occurs in cross-attention and describe at least one architecture-level fix."
> *Looking for:* Prompt neglect mechanism: CLIP text encoder processes the prompt as a sequence; the cross-attention in the U-Net/DiT attends to text token representations. Some tokens receive consistently low attention weight — they don't significantly influence the denoising. This happens when: (1) the model has learned to give high weight to visually-dominant tokens (colors, objects, styles) and low weight to less visually-salient tokens (adjectives, prepositions, rare entities), (2) the order of tokens creates priority — tokens later in the prompt may receive less attention due to position bias. Fix: Prompt-to-Prompt (P2P) directly controls attention maps — you can up-weight specific tokens' attention. Attend-and-Excite (A&E) adds a regularization term during inference that maximizes the maximum attention activation for specified tokens, ensuring they influence generation. Strong hire also mentions that T5 text encoders (used in SD3, FLUX) outperform CLIP for prompt following because T5 has richer semantic representations and better handles complex syntactic structures.

### Area 3: DDIM Deterministic Sampling — What Skip Steps Actually Means

**Surface:** "DDIM runs in 20 steps instead of 1000. How?"

**Probe 1:** "DDPM samples x_{t-1} from a Gaussian posterior q(x_{t-1}|x_t, x_0). DDIM bypasses this posterior. Mathematically, what does DDIM use instead, and why does this enable larger step sizes?"
> *Looking for:* DDPM posterior: q(x_{t-1}|x_t, x_0) is a Gaussian with specific mean and variance. This must be small steps to maintain the Markov chain's accuracy — taking a step from t=500 directly to t=100 violates the sequential Markov assumption. DDIM: reformulates the reverse process as a deterministic ODE (ordinary differential equation) rather than a stochastic SDE. The DDIM update rule: x_{t-1} = √(ᾱ_{t-1}) * x̂_0(x_t) + √(1-ᾱ_{t-1}) * ε_θ(x_t, t), where x̂_0(x_t) is the predicted clean image from the noise prediction. This update is deterministic (no random noise added) and the step size is controlled by the difference in ᾱ values, not by the discrete timestep. Key: the DDIM trajectory is consistent regardless of which subset of timesteps you use — you can skip from t=500 directly to t=100 without cumulative error because each step is a valid ODE integration step.

**Probe 2:** "DDIM determinism enables 'inversion' — you can map a real image back to its noise. What is DDIM inversion, how is it used in image editing applications, and what are its failure modes?"
> *Looking for:* DDIM inversion: run DDIM forward (adding noise) instead of backward. Starting from a real image x_0, apply the DDIM update in reverse to estimate x_1, x_2, ..., x_T. The resulting x_T is approximately the noise that, when denoised with the same model and prompt, would reconstruct x_0. Image editing: edit the inverted noise (by changing the conditioning prompt or applying attention map edits), then denoise to get the edited image. The original structure is preserved because the same noise is used. Failure modes: (1) accumulation of discretization error — each inversion step introduces small errors that compound over T steps. For large T, x_T is not exactly the noise that produces x_0. (2) CFG during inversion makes inversion less accurate — the guidance amplifies errors. Null-text inversion (Mokady et al.) addresses this by optimizing a null-text embedding per timestep to minimize reconstruction error. Strong hire knows that DDIM inversion is the foundation of real image editing with diffusion models.

**Expert probe:** "Flow matching is an alternative to DDPM that uses a simpler ODE with straight-line trajectories. Compare the DDPM loss, DDIM sampling, and flow matching on three dimensions: training signal quality, inference speed, and inversion quality. When would you prefer flow matching over DDPM for production systems?"
> *Looking for:* DDPM training signal: MSE on noise prediction, with a complex SNR-dependent weighting across timesteps (improved by Min-SNR). Inference: stochastic (SDE) or deterministic (DDIM ODE). DDIM inversion: moderately accurate, degrades with CFG. Flow matching: trains to predict the velocity field (direction from noise to clean data) along straight-line paths. Simpler training signal (uniform weighting across timesteps). Inference: solve a straight-line ODE — the trajectory between noise and clean image is approximately a straight line (fewer integration steps needed). Inversion: more accurate because the ODE is simpler (straight line ≈ easier to integrate backwards). Inference speed: flow matching trajectories are straighter → need fewer NFE (number of function evaluations) to reach high quality. SD3 and FLUX use flow matching for this reason. Production preference: flow matching for systems that need fast inference (4-8 steps) with high quality. DDPM for compatibility with existing ControlNets and LoRA adapters trained for DDPM-based models.

### Area 4: U-Net vs. DiT — Scaling Behavior and Architecture Tradeoffs

**Surface:** "When should you use DiT over U-Net?"

**Probe 1:** "The README states DiT follows Transformer scaling laws. Provide a concrete FLOPs comparison: a U-Net model for 512×512 latent diffusion (standard SD v1.5) vs. a DiT model at the same parameter count. Which is more compute-efficient at different scales, and why do scaling laws favor DiT?"
> *Looking for:* U-Net at 512×512 with 8× VAE compression → 64×64 latent grid. U-Net FLOPs: dominated by convolutional layers at each resolution level. FLOPs scale as O(HW × C²) per layer (C = channel size). Adding more layers increases FLOPs but also adds capacity efficiently. DiT: processes the 64×64 latent as 4×4 patch tokens → (64/4)² = 256 tokens. Transformer FLOPs per layer: O(N² × D + N × D²). For N=256 tokens, FLOPs per layer ≈ 256² × D + 256 × D². Scaling: DiT follows the Chinchilla-like scaling law — doubling parameters reliably improves quality. U-Net scaling is less predictable (adding layers at specific resolutions doesn't always help). DiT wins at large scale (>500M params) because Transformer infrastructure (FlashAttention, tensor parallelism, MoE) is much more mature. Strong candidate states that at small model sizes (<200M params), U-Net is competitive; at large scales (>1B params), DiT dominates.

**Probe 2:** "One practical advantage of DiT over U-Net is handling variable resolution and duration. Explain exactly how DiT handles a 1024×768 image vs. a 1280×720 image without retraining, while U-Net struggles with this."
> *Looking for:* DiT with 3D patchification: the image is split into patches (e.g., 2×2 or 4×4). For 1024×768: patches = (1024/4) × (768/4) = 256 × 192 = 49,152 patches. For 1280×720: patches = 320 × 180 = 57,600 patches. The number of tokens varies, but the Transformer's attention mechanism is sequence-length agnostic — it operates on any-length sequences. RoPE positional encoding generalizes to unseen positions. U-Net problem: convolutional layers at each resolution level are designed for specific aspect ratios. Skip connections between encoder and decoder require matching spatial dimensions. A U-Net trained on square 512×512 images has hardcoded intermediate spatial dimensions; a non-square input produces mismatched dimensions at skip connections. Fix: U-Net can be extended with "arbitrary resolution training" but requires significant architectural modifications. Strong hire explains that DiT's flexibility was a key factor in Sora's variable-duration video generation.

**Expert probe:** "CLIP as text encoder vs. T5 as text encoder: CLIP was trained contrastively on image-text pairs; T5 was trained autoregressively on text only. For a text-to-image system, explain what information each encoder captures and loses, and provide a concrete example of a prompt where T5 produces better conditioning than CLIP and one where CLIP does."
> *Looking for:* CLIP captures: visual-semantic correspondences — concepts that co-occur with visual content in image-text pairs. Very good at: object names, styles, colors, visual attributes. CLIP loses: abstract concepts, complex syntax, rare entities not well-represented in image-text pairs, relational reasoning ("a person who is taller than the building"). T5 captures: rich linguistic structure, syntax, semantics from large text-only pretraining. Very good at: complex prompts, negations ("a car without wheels"), relative clauses, rare words. T5 loses: visual-domain specificity — it doesn't know that "Rembrandt lighting" implies specific shadow patterns because it learned from text only. T5 example where it wins: "A red house on the left of a blue house with a tree in front" — complex spatial relational reasoning. CLIP example where it wins: "In the style of Studio Ghibli, a forest spirit" — CLIP was trained on art style descriptions paired with images. Strong hire mentions that SD3 uses both CLIP and T5 encoders together — concatenated text embeddings — to get the benefits of both.

### Area 5: Safety and NSFW Filtering

**Surface:** "How do you prevent the system from generating harmful images?"

**Probe 1:** "Your prompt filter uses a text classifier to block NSFW prompts before generation. A user submits 'a physician examining a patient.' Your classifier blocks it. What's happening, and how do you fix the false positive rate without increasing false negative rate?"
> *Looking for:* The classifier may be triggering on co-occurrence patterns in its training data: 'examining' or anatomical terms may have appeared in NSFW training examples. False positive problem in safety classifiers: precision-recall tradeoff. If the threshold is set very conservatively to catch all NSFW (high recall), benign medical/artistic queries are blocked (low precision). Fix: (1) context-aware classification that considers the full query semantics, not just keyword co-occurrence. (2) Hard negative training: add labeled benign examples of medical, artistic, and scientific descriptions that contain anatomy-adjacent language. (3) Multi-level filtering: a lightweight classifier blocks clear NSFW; ambiguous cases go to a more expensive but more accurate classifier or human review. Strong candidate also discusses that the base model itself was trained to refuse certain concepts via the attention guidance technique ("Erasing concepts" in SD).

**Probe 2:** "C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity) watermarking embeds an invisible signal in generated images. Explain how invisible watermarking works technically, what attacks can remove it, and whether you'd rely on it as your primary safety mechanism."
> *Looking for:* Invisible watermarking: embeds a high-frequency signal in the image that is imperceptible to humans but detectable by a decoder model. Common approaches: (1) Least-significant bit embedding in pixel space (fragile — lossy compression removes it), (2) Learned watermarks (HiDDeN, Stable Signature) that embed in the DCT or feature space — more robust to JPEG compression and resizing. Attack vectors: (1) JPEG compression at quality < 85, (2) Resizing with interpolation, (3) Adversarial attacks designed to remove watermarks, (4) GAN-based watermark removal. Why not primary mechanism: watermarks can be removed and don't prevent generation. Role: evidence gathering (prove an image was AI-generated for legal/regulatory purposes), not prevention. Primary mechanism: output classifier + prompt filter + rate limiting + user authentication. Strong hire distinguishes content provenance (C2PA) from harm prevention — different goals.

**Expert probe:** "Negative prompting ('do not generate X') is a technique to steer away from unwanted content. Explain the mechanism by which negative prompts work in CFG, and why it's insufficient as a safety mechanism — providing a concrete attack vector."
> *Looking for:* Negative prompt mechanism: replace the unconditional generation in CFG with a negative-conditioned generation. The guidance formula becomes: ε_guided = ε_neg_prompt + w * (ε_cond - ε_neg_prompt). This pushes the generation away from the negative prompt's direction in the diffusion trajectory. Limitation: the negative prompt defines an exclusion direction in CLIP embedding space. Attack vector: (1) Rephrase the desired harmful content so its CLIP embedding is far from the negated embedding — use synonyms, indirect descriptions, or fictional framings. (2) Jailbreak via style transfer: request the content in a style that shifts the CLIP embedding (e.g., "in the style of a famous artist" can encode concepts far from the prohibited term's embedding). (3) Multi-step attack: first generate a neutral base image, then use inpainting to progressively add prohibited content in small steps, each below the classifier threshold. Strong hire notes that negative prompting is a useful creative tool but must be combined with output classifiers for safety.

## Red Flags to Watch For

- **MSE on noise = the ELBO.** Cannot derive the connection or explain the simplification steps.
- **"High guidance scale = sharper images."** Does not understand why very high w causes artifacts (out-of-manifold artifacts, not just sharpness).
- **DDIM is just fewer steps.** Cannot explain the ODE formulation or why it enables consistent subsampling.
- **DiT vs. U-Net is just architecture preference.** Cannot give the scaling law argument or FLOPs comparison.
- **Watermarks prevent image misuse.** Does not understand the difference between content provenance and harm prevention.
- **CLIP text encoder is always sufficient.** Cannot explain what CLIP misses for complex prompts.
