# Chapter 11: Interviewer Guide — Text-to-Video (Sora/Video DiT)

## Opening Problem Statement

"Design a text-to-video generation system at Sora's capability level — 4K resolution, up to 60 seconds, physically plausible motion. I want to go deep on the spatiotemporal VAE compression bottleneck, the attention complexity tradeoffs of full vs. factorized space-time attention, temporal consistency failure modes and their automatic detection, and your honest assessment of what Sora learned vs. what it fundamentally cannot do. This is the hardest problem in generative AI — I expect Staff-level reasoning about the physical and computational limits."

## Probing Question Tree

### Area 1: Spatiotemporal VAE — Why Temporal Compression Is Limited

**Surface:** "The README explains the video VAE compresses 8× spatially and 8× temporally. Why can't you compress more temporally?"

**Probe 1:** "A 10-second video at 24fps = 240 frames. With 8× temporal compression → 30 latent frames. With 16× temporal compression → 15 latent frames. Why does aggressive temporal compression (32× or more) degrade video generation quality in ways that spatial compression at the same ratio does not?"
> *Looking for:* The key difference: spatial compression (8×) maps 8×8 pixel blocks to one latent — local spatial redundancy is high and each block is partially independent. Temporal compression: maps multiple frames to one latent — but frames in a dynamic scene can change dramatically frame-to-frame (fast motion, scene cuts). At 32× temporal compression: a model trained on 24fps video compresses every 32 frames (1.3 seconds) to one latent code. Fast motion (a running athlete, explosion) within that 1.3-second window is irrecoverably lost. Spatial compression has no equivalent because spatially adjacent pixels are statistically highly correlated. Temporal: correlation between frames decreases with object motion speed and scene complexity. Strong candidate notes the fundamental tradeoff: high temporal compression only works for slow-motion or static-background video.

**Probe 2:** "Temporal convolutions in the VAE encoder must learn to capture motion patterns across frames. Compare using 3D convolutions (kernel: T×H×W) vs. factorized temporal convolutions (1×H×W spatial + T×1×1 temporal) in the VAE encoder. What motion patterns does each miss, and which is used in practice?"
> *Looking for:* 3D convolutions: capture spatiotemporal patterns jointly — a 3×3×3 kernel sees motion (how a 3×3 spatial window changes across 3 consecutive frames) in one operation. Captures: complex spatiotemporal patterns like diagonal motion, scale changes. Cost: O(T×H×W×C²) per convolution. Factorized: separate spatial conv (processes each frame independently) + temporal conv (processes each spatial position independently across time). Misses: interactions between spatial changes and temporal changes within a single kernel (e.g., an object moving diagonally requires both spatial and temporal information simultaneously). Cost: O((HW + T)×C²) per factorized block — much cheaper. In practice: factorized temporal convolutions are the standard (used in Imagen Video, Movie Gen) because 3D convolutions are too expensive at video resolution. The factorized model is a good approximation because most natural motion is approximately factorizable into a spatial component and a temporal component.

**Expert probe:** "Aliasing in the temporal domain occurs when a video VAE is trained on 24fps videos but deployed on 60fps videos. Explain the Nyquist criterion for temporal compression, what temporal aliasing looks like in generated videos, and how production video generation systems handle the variable-framerate problem."
> *Looking for:* Nyquist criterion: to faithfully represent motion at frequency f (cycles/second), you need sampling rate > 2f. With 8× temporal compression, the Nyquist-limited frequency is f_max = 24fps/(2×8) = 1.5 cycles/second — motion faster than 1.5 cycles/second causes aliasing. At 60fps: the Nyquist limit is 3.75 cycles/second — you can represent faster motion before aliasing. Temporal aliasing: high-speed motion (ball in flight, fast-moving object) appears jerky or reverses direction (temporal stroboscopic effect). Variable framerate handling: (1) Train the VAE on mixed-framerate video (24fps, 30fps, 60fps) with frame rate as a conditioning signal. (2) Implement temporal interpolation before compression (downsample to 24fps before the VAE) — loses high-frequency motion. (3) Use frame rate-aware temporal compression ratios. Strong hire notes that Sora's technical report explicitly conditions on the frame rate and uses a flexible spatiotemporal patchification.

### Area 2: Factorized vs. Full Space-Time Attention — FLOPs and What You Lose

**Surface:** "The README mentions factorized attention. Why not use full space-time attention?"

**Probe 1:** "For a video with T=16 frames, H×W = 16×16 latent spatial tokens per frame, total tokens N = T×H×W = 4096. Compare the FLOPs for: (a) full space-time attention, (b) factorized spatial + temporal attention. Show the calculation."
> *Looking for:* Full space-time attention: FLOPs = 2 × N² × D = 2 × 4096² × D = 33.5M × D per layer. For D=1024: 34 GFLOPs per layer. For 24 layers: ~816 GFLOPs just for attention. Factorized: spatial attention (within each frame): FLOPs = T × 2 × (H×W)² × D = 16 × 2 × 256² × D = 2M × D per layer. Temporal attention (each spatial position across frames): FLOPs = H×W × 2 × T² × D = 256 × 2 × 256 × D = 131K × D per layer. Total factorized: (2M + 131K) × D ≈ 2.1M × D — 16× cheaper than full attention. Full attention captures: direct spatial-temporal interactions (position (h₁,w₁) at time t₁ can directly attend to position (h₂,w₂) at time t₂). Factorized loses: cross-frame spatial interactions are handled by the combination of spatial (within frame) and temporal (same position across frames) but cannot capture "object at (h₁,w₁) in frame 1 interacts with object at (h₂,w₂) in frame 5 at a distance."

**Probe 2:** "Full space-time attention can capture long-range spatiotemporal dependencies that factorized attention cannot. For which types of video content does factorized attention fail noticeably compared to full attention?"
> *Looking for:* Factorized fails for: (1) Complex occlusion: an object disappears behind another and reappears later at a different spatial position. Factorized temporal attention only tracks the same (h,w) position across frames — it can't track the object's position change. (2) Camera motion: panning, zooming causes every pixel's temporal trajectory to change — factorized temporal attention at each fixed (h,w) sees a different object at each timestep (background slides through). Full attention can capture the global motion context. (3) Object interaction: two objects at different positions interact (a ball hitting a wall) — factorized temporal attention at the ball's position doesn't see the wall's temporal context. (4) Scene cuts: an abrupt cut requires the model to understand that frames before and after the cut are independent — factorized temporal attention at the cut frame incorrectly tries to interpolate between visually unrelated frames.

**Expert probe:** "Video diffusion models must decide whether to process time as a batch dimension (process each frame independently with spatial attention) or as a sequence dimension (process frames sequentially with temporal attention). There is a third option: window attention that processes a local temporal window. Derive the FLOPs for window attention with window size w_t and compare to factorized, then argue when each is optimal."
> *Looking for:* Window attention: for each spatial position, instead of attending to all T frames (cost T²), attend only to a temporal window of size w_t. FLOPs = H×W × 2 × w_t² × D per layer. For w_t=4, H×W=256, D=1024: 256 × 2 × 16 × 1024 = 8M per layer — between factorized (2.1M) and full (34G). Window is optimal when: (1) temporal dependencies are local (most natural video motion is locally continuous), (2) T >> w_t (long videos where full attention is prohibitive). Window vs. factorized: window captures local spatiotemporal interactions (nearby frames at nearby positions) that factorized misses, at moderate additional cost. Full attention only when: short video clips (T ≤ 8) where the FLOPs are manageable, or for high-value content where quality matters most. Strong hire mentions that sliding window + global attention (a few global tokens attend to all frames) is a common practical compromise used in video transformers.

### Area 3: Temporal Consistency Failure Modes and Automatic Detection

**Surface:** "What is temporal consistency and how do you measure it?"

**Probe 1:** "A generated video shows a person walking, and their shirt changes color from blue to red between frame 47 and frame 48. This is a temporal inconsistency. Describe three automated metrics that would catch this, and explain what signal each uses."
> *Looking for:* (1) Warped frame similarity: use optical flow to warp frame 47 forward to frame 48, then compute LPIPS between the warped frame 47 and actual frame 48. If LPIPS is high, the temporal change is inconsistent with the predicted motion. (2) Feature vector cosine similarity: extract DINO or CLIP image features for each consecutive frame pair. A sudden drop in cosine similarity between frame 47 and 48 indicates a visually discontinuous change. (3) Semantic consistency (CLIP-based): compute the CLIP embedding of each frame and track its trajectory. A sudden jump in the CLIP embedding space indicates a semantic change (color shift, identity change). Additionally: VBench's 'Subject Consistency' metric specifically measures whether the main subject's appearance is consistent across frames using a combination of feature similarity and optical flow consistency.

**Probe 2:** "Temporal consistency is also violated in subtle ways that don't involve discontinuous jumps — for example, a generated crowd scene where people's faces flicker (slightly different face each frame without a sharp cut). Why does flickering happen despite the model using temporal attention, and how would you diagnose the root cause?"
> *Looking for:* Flickering root cause: (1) Insufficient temporal attention range — if the temporal attention window is too narrow (e.g., w_t=2), frames outside the window don't influence each other, allowing independent mode variation. (2) High frequency components in the diffusion process — the denoising steps at early timesteps (high noise) are mostly independent per frame; the temporal consistency is built gradually. If the number of denoising steps is too few, high-frequency details (face textures) don't have enough time to be made temporally consistent. (3) VAE temporal compression artifacts — the VAE encodes 8 frames into one latent, but the intra-chunk consistency (frames 1-7 vs. frames 8-15) may differ at chunk boundaries. Diagnosis: (1) Compute per-pixel variance across time for static objects — high temporal variance in static background = flickering. (2) Check VBench 'Temporal Flickering' score. (3) Ablation: increase temporal attention window size and compare.

**Expert probe:** "Physical plausibility is a temporal consistency problem that goes beyond appearance — a video of water flowing uphill is temporally consistent (no flickering, smooth flow) but physically implausible. How do you measure physical plausibility automatically, and what has Sora demonstrably learned vs. not learned about physics?"
> *Looking for:* Automatic physical plausibility metrics: (1) Velocity field consistency — estimate optical flow and check if it satisfies physical constraints (conservation of mass for fluids, rigid body motion constraints for objects). (2) Acceleration profiles — real physics has smooth acceleration; abrupt velocity changes indicate physically implausible events. (3) Horizon line consistency — for camera motion, the horizon should move predictably. (4) Shadow direction consistency — light direction should be constant within a scene. What Sora has learned: trajectory statistics of common objects (balls roll, people walk), general dynamic scene composition, camera motion styles. What Sora hasn't learned reliably: (1) strict conservation laws (fluid simulation, rigid body collision dynamics), (2) causal physical chains (pushing an object should move it predictably), (3) long-horizon physical consistency (an object placed on a table should remain there 30 seconds later if the scene is static). Strong hire notes that Sora is a statistical learner — it has learned correlations between visual patterns and physical-looking motion, not a physical simulator.

### Area 4: Joint Image-Video Training — Weight Sharing and Gradient Conflicts

**Surface:** "Why use joint image-video training? How does weight sharing work?"

**Probe 1:** "Images are treated as single-frame videos in joint training. The temporal attention layers see sequences of length T=1 (images) and T=16 (video clips) in the same training batch. Explain the gradient dynamics: do image gradients and video gradients reinforce or conflict with each other in the spatial attention layers?"
> *Looking for:* Spatial attention layers: process each frame independently. For images (T=1) and videos (T=16), the spatial attention sees the same per-frame computation — these gradients are aligned. More image data helps the spatial attention learn better visual semantics and quality. Temporal attention layers: for T=1 (images), temporal attention has nothing to do (attend to just one frame). Images contribute zero gradient to temporal attention weights. This is a problem: temporal attention only receives gradient updates from video data, which is much scarcer. Gradient conflict scenario: during video training, the temporal attention tries to maintain temporal consistency. But if image and video share the same feature space, image batch gradients from the spatial layers may shift the representation in ways that make temporal coherence harder. Mitigation: weight the losses differently (higher weight for video data in temporal layers), or use separate sets of normalization parameters for temporal vs. spatial layers.

**Probe 2:** "Movie Gen (Meta, 2024) found that joint image-video training outperformed video-only training with the same total training steps. What theoretical reason explains why images help, and what is the limit of this benefit?"
> *Looking for:* Why images help: images provide (1) a much larger diversity of visual content (5B+ image-text pairs vs. ~100M video clips), (2) fine-grained visual quality training signal at the pixel level (high-quality images with rich captions improve the model's visual vocabulary), (3) concept coverage — rare visual concepts (specific art styles, unusual animals, complex scenes) appear in image data but rarely in video. The spatial DiT layers benefit directly. Limit of the benefit: (1) diminishing returns — images can improve the model's visual quality but cannot teach it temporal dynamics. At some scale of video data, the temporal quality gains from more video outweigh additional image data. (2) Distribution mismatch — high-quality images (photos, art) have a different distribution than video frames (slightly blurry due to motion, compressed JPEG, different aspect ratios). Aggressive image pretraining may shift the model toward unnatural video characteristics. Movie Gen uses a curriculum that gradually shifts from image-heavy to video-heavy training.

**Expert probe:** "Sora uses a Video DiT with full space-time attention but operates on compressed tokens from a spatiotemporal VAE. Given that full space-time attention is O(N²) and N grows as T×H×W, estimate the maximum video length Sora could generate in one batch on 8 H100 GPUs, and explain the memory bottleneck."
> *Looking for:* Sora's video DiT: operates on latent tokens after VAE compression. Assuming 4× temporal, 8× spatial compression: a 10-second video at 30fps = 300 frames → 75 latent frames. At 1024×576 input → 128×72 latent spatial → 9216 spatial positions. Total N = 75 × 9216 = 691,200 tokens. Full self-attention: N² × D = (691,200)² × 4096 ≈ 2×10¹⁸ FLOPs per layer — completely intractable. Sora uses factorized or windowed attention with selective full-attention. Memory bottleneck: KV-cache for attention = N × D × layers = 691,200 × 4096 × 48 layers × 2 bytes (FP16) ≈ 270 GB — exceeds 8 H100 GPU memory (640 GB total but with model weights occupying ~100-200 GB). Maximum video length given actual Sora constraints: inference is done in smaller chunks with sliding window attention. Strong hire notes that the 60-second Sora videos are likely generated with a cascade pipeline (low-res base → temporal SR → spatial SR) that never processes the full 60-second video as a single attention window.

### Area 5: FVD vs. FID — What I3D Captures

**Surface:** "The README introduces FVD. How is it different from FID?"

**Probe 1:** "FVD uses I3D (Inflated 3D ConvNet) features trained on Kinetics action recognition. A generated video that is visually high-quality but has unrealistic motion (water flowing sideways) — would FVD catch this? Why or why not?"
> *Looking for:* FVD compares the distribution of I3D features. I3D was trained to classify human actions — it's sensitive to motion patterns that look like recognizable human activities. Water flowing sideways: I3D's features are primarily tuned for human body motion patterns, not fluid dynamics. Physically implausible fluid motion may still produce I3D features similar to real water videos if the visual texture and overall motion pattern are similar. FVD would likely NOT catch this because: (1) I3D doesn't model physical plausibility, (2) the distribution of I3D features for 'realistic-looking but physically wrong water' may overlap with 'real water.' FVD is sensitive to: (1) appearance quality of each frame (via spatial I3D features), (2) temporal coherence of human action patterns, (3) large-scale motion patterns (pan, zoom). FVD is insensitive to: physical plausibility of non-human motion, fine-grained object consistency, semantic accuracy of generated content.

**Probe 2:** "FVD requires a large number of videos (at least 2048, ideally 10K+) for reliable estimation. For a video generation team doing rapid iteration (training runs every few days), what is a practical lightweight proxy for FVD that gives faster signal?"
> *Looking for:* Lightweight alternatives: (1) Per-frame FID: compute FID on individual sampled frames — fast, correlates with per-frame quality but misses temporal dynamics. Run every checkpoint. (2) CLIP score per frame averaged over video: measures text-video alignment efficiently. (3) Temporal consistency proxy: compute DINO feature cosine similarity between consecutive frame pairs for a small sample (50-100 videos). High average = good temporal consistency, no FVD computation needed. (4) Optical flow magnitude variance: compute optical flow for generated vs. real videos; compare the distribution of flow magnitudes as a proxy for motion plausibility. Fast and model-free. Production practice: use per-frame FID + temporal consistency as early-signal metrics during training, compute full FVD + human evaluation for milestone checkpoints (weekly or before launch). VBench multi-dimensional evaluation is the most comprehensive but slowest.

**Expert probe:** "FVD was proposed in 2018 and uses Kinetics-pretrained I3D. In 2024, newer video understanding models (VideoMAE, TimeSformer trained on much larger datasets) exist. Design a better video generation evaluation metric than FVD, considering: (1) what features to use, (2) how to handle the temporal vs. spatial decomposition, and (3) how to include text-video alignment."
> *Looking for:* Improved video metric design: (1) Features: use a model pretrained on diverse video understanding tasks (VideoMAE-2 trained on Kinetics-700 + AudioSet + in-the-wild video) that captures motion, scene dynamics, and temporal patterns more broadly than I3D. (2) Temporal vs. spatial: compute FD (Frechet Distance) separately on (a) frame-level features (spatial quality), (b) temporal pooled features (dynamic quality), (c) trajectory features (motion consistency). Report all three independently, allowing diagnosis of which dimension is failing. (3) Text-video alignment: compute video-text cosine similarity using a model trained on video-text pairs (e.g., VideoCLIP, InternVideo) rather than image-text CLIP, which doesn't understand temporal dynamics. Composite metric: weighted combination of spatial FD + temporal FD + text-video alignment score. Human eval correlation: validate that the composite metric correlates better with human preferences than FVD on a held-out benchmark. Strong hire notes this is an active research area and there's no consensus on the best metric — any production system should combine multiple metrics and human evaluation.

## Red Flags to Watch For

- **"Just extend image diffusion to video."** Doesn't identify the specific challenges of temporal compression and attention complexity.
- **Factorized attention captures all temporal dependencies.** Cannot identify what factorized attention misses (non-local spatiotemporal interactions).
- **FVD is sufficient for video quality evaluation.** Doesn't know its blind spots (physical plausibility, semantic accuracy).
- **Sora generates 60-second videos in a single forward pass.** Doesn't account for the computational impossibility and assume cascade pipeline.
- **Temporal consistency = no frame jumping.** Only thinks of hard discontinuities, not flickering or physical plausibility.
- **Image training always helps video.** Cannot identify the limit of the benefit or the distribution mismatch issue.
